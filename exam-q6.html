Question 6 · 5 Marks

HOG Orientation Binning & RANSAC

📄 Original question

Q6(a) Consider the 9-point bin in the Histogram of Oriented Gradients (HOG) technique. Determine the bin boundaries and their respective representation level. Compute the histogram values for the pixel with a direction of 57 degrees. 3M

Q6(b) What is the RANSAC algorithm and mention a few of its applications in computer vision. 2M

Solution — Q6(a)

HOG uses 9 bins spanning the unsigned-gradient range 0°–180°, so each bin is \(180/9 = 20°\) wide.

Bin #BoundariesRepresentation level (centre)
10°–20°0°
220°–40°20°
340°–60°40°
460°–80°60°
580°–100°80°
6100°–120°100°
7120°–140°120°
8140°–160°140°
9160°–180°160° (180°≡0°)

(Following the course's convention, the representation levels are taken at the multiples of 20°: 0, 20, 40, …, 160.) A direction of 57° lies between the 40° and 60° representation levels, so its magnitude is split between those two bins by linear interpolation (distance weighting):

Weight to the 40° bin
\(\dfrac{60-57}{20} = \dfrac{3}{20} = 0.15\)
Weight to the 60° bin
\(\dfrac{57-40}{20} = \dfrac{17}{20} = 0.85\)
Distribute the magnitude |g|
Bin 40° gets \(+0.15\,|g|\);   Bin 60° gets \(+0.85\,|g|\); all other bins 0.
Answer

57° contributes 0.15·|g| to the 40° bin and 0.85·|g| to the 60° bin. For example, if the gradient magnitude is \(|g|=85\): \(\;\)Bin40 \(= 12.75\), Bin60 \(= 72.25\), rest \(=0\).

Simpler "hard" binning (no interpolation) would dump the whole magnitude into the single bin that contains 57°, i.e. the 40°–60° bin — state whichever method your instructor expects.

Solution — Q6(b) — RANSAC

RANSAC = RANdom SAmple Consensus — a robust model-fitting method that ignores outliers by repeatedly fitting to small random samples and keeping the model that the most data points agree with.

Sample
Randomly select the minimum number of points needed to define the model (2 for a line, 4 for a homography).
Solve
Fit the model to that minimal sample.
Score
Count inliers — points within a preset distance threshold of the model.
Repeat & keep best
Iterate; keep the model with the most inliers, then optionally re-fit by least squares on all inliers.
Applications

Image alignment / panorama stitching (homography estimation), 3D reconstruction & stereo (fundamental/essential matrix), motion/translation estimation and tracking, line/plane fitting, and removing mismatched feature correspondences after SIFT matching. Works reliably when outliers are < 50%.

Practice questions

P6.1 A pixel has direction \(95°\) and magnitude 40. Split its vote across the 9 HOG bins. 2M

95° lies between representation levels 80° and 100°.

Weights
to 80°: \((100-95)/20 = 0.25\);   to 100°: \((95-80)/20 = 0.75\)
Distribute magnitude 40
Bin 80° \(= 0.25\times40 = 10\);   Bin 100° \(= 0.75\times40 = 30\)
Answer

Bin 80° = 10, Bin 100° = 30, all others 0.

P6.2 Where does a pixel at exactly \(30°\) (magnitude \(|g|\)) go? 1M
30° is midway between 20° and 40°
to 20°: \((40-30)/20=0.5\); to 40°: \((30-20)/20=0.5\)
Answer

Split equally: \(0.5|g|\) to the 20° bin and \(0.5|g|\) to the 40° bin.

P6.3 A pixel at \(170°\) — show how the wrap-around works. 2M

170° is between the 160° level and 180°, but 180° ≡ 0° for unsigned gradients, so it wraps to the 0° bin.

Weights
to 160°: \((180-170)/20 = 0.5\);   to 0° (the wrapped 180°): \((170-160)/20 = 0.5\)
Answer

Half the magnitude to bin 160°, half to bin 0° — the histogram is circular over 0–180°.

P6.4 Derive the length of the full HOG feature vector for a 64×128 detection window (8×8 cells, 2×2 blocks, 9 bins). 2M
Cells
\(64/8 = 8\) across, \(128/8 = 16\) down → \(8\times16\) cells
Blocks (2×2 cells, stride 1, overlapping)
\((8-1)\times(16-1) = 7\times15 = 105\) blocks
Per block
\(2\times2\) cells \(\times 9\) bins \(= 36\) values
Total
\(105 \times 36 = \mathbf{3780}\)
Answer

3780-dimensional (= 7×15 blocks × 4 cells × 9 bins).

P6.5 RANSAC: with 20% outliers and a minimal sample of \(s=2\), roughly how many iterations \(N\) are needed for 99% success? 2M

Use \(N = \dfrac{\log(1-p)}{\log\!\big(1-(1-e)^{s}\big)}\) with \(p=0.99\), \(e=0.2\), \(s=2\).

Prob. a sample is all-inliers
\((1-e)^s = 0.8^2 = 0.64\)
Plug in
\(N = \dfrac{\log(0.01)}{\log(1-0.64)} = \dfrac{-2}{\log(0.36)} = \dfrac{-2}{-0.4437} \approx 4.5\)
Answer

About 5 iterations. (Few outliers ⇒ very few rounds; the count climbs steeply as the outlier ratio or sample size grows.)

P6.6 Why does RANSAC beat plain least-squares when there are outliers? 1M
Answer

Least-squares minimises squared error over all points, so a few far-off outliers drag the fit toward them. RANSAC instead fits to small all-inlier samples and scores by inlier count (not squared error), so outliers simply fail to vote and never corrupt the chosen model.
