SIFT & RANSAC

How to extract local features that survive scale and rotation (SIFT), and how to fit a model when half your matches are wrong (RANSAC). Together they power panorama stitching, object recognition and visual SLAM.

Why extract local features?

Take panorama stitching: we have two overlapping photos — how do we combine them? The recipe is extract features → match features → align images. The same three-step recipe drives 3D reconstruction, motion tracking for AR, object recognition, image retrieval, and robot/car navigation (visual SLAM).

◆ Advantages of local features
  • Locality — local patches are robust to occlusion and clutter.
  • Quantity — hundreds or thousands per image, so matching is redundant and reliable.
  • Distinctiveness — a good descriptor can be told apart across a huge database.
  • Efficiency — fast enough for real-time use.

Invariance and the three components

A good feature should be detectable again after the image changes. We want:

  • Geometric invariance: translation, rotation, scale.
  • Photometric invariance: brightness, exposure, contrast.

Every local-feature method has the same three components:

ComponentJob
DetectionFind the interest points (where? — repeatably).
DescriptionExtract a vector descriptor around each interest point (what does it look like?).
MatchingFind correspondences between descriptors in two views.

Harris (Session 6) is a great detector but is not scale-invariant. SIFT solves detection and description across scale and rotation.

SIFT — Scale-Invariant Feature Transform

Introduced by David Lowe (UBC, 2004). Its goal: extract distinctive features that can be matched against a large database, with invariance to scale and rotation and robustness to affine distortion, 3D-viewpoint change, noise, and illumination change. The pipeline has four stages:

Scale-space extrema detection
Search over both location and scale for stable keypoints, using a Difference-of-Gaussians pyramid.
Keypoint localization
Refine each candidate's position/scale and reject low-contrast points and points lying on edges (poorly localized).
Orientation assignment
Assign a dominant orientation from the local gradient histogram so the descriptor can be measured relative to it → rotation invariance.
Keypoint descriptor
Build a gradient-orientation descriptor in the rotated, scaled frame — the famous 128-D vector (4×4 spatial grid × 8 orientation bins).

Scale-space & Difference of Gaussians

An object's "right" amount of blur depends on how big it appears. SIFT builds a scale-space: the image repeatedly blurred by Gaussians of increasing \(\sigma\), grouped into octaves (each octave halves the resolution).

\[ D(x,y,\sigma) = \big(G(x,y,k\sigma) - G(x,y,\sigma)\big) * I(x,y) \]

The Difference of Gaussians (DoG) — subtracting adjacent blur levels — is a cheap approximation to the scale-normalised Laplacian-of-Gaussian, a strong blob detector. A pixel that is a local extremum among its 26 neighbours (8 in its scale + 9 above + 9 below in scale) is a candidate keypoint. Its scale tells you the blob's characteristic size — the foundation of scale invariance.

◆ Why DoG instead of LoG?

The LoG is the ideal blob detector but expensive. DoG ≈ LoG and is almost free once you have the Gaussian pyramid (just subtractions). Extrema in the 3-D \((x, y, \sigma)\) volume are blobs at their natural scale.

Orientation & descriptor → rotation invariance

At each keypoint, SIFT computes a histogram of local gradient orientations and picks the dominant peak as the keypoint's canonical orientation. All subsequent measurements are made relative to this orientation, so rotating the image doesn't change the descriptor. The descriptor itself is a grid of orientation histograms (4×4×8 = 128 numbers), then normalised for illumination invariance. The result: features that match reliably across scale, rotation, viewpoint and lighting — even on Mars-rover images.

RANSAC — fitting amid outliers

After matching, some correspondences are wrong (outliers). Plain least-squares is wrecked by even a few outliers because it tries to satisfy everyone. RANSAC (RANdom SAmple Consensus) fits robustly:

Sample
Randomly pick the minimum number of points to define the model (2 for a line, 4 for a homography).
Solve
Fit the model to just that minimal sample.
Score
Count inliers — points within a preset distance threshold of the model.
Repeat & keep best
Iterate many times; keep the model with the most inliers. Optionally re-fit (least squares) using all inliers at the end.
◆ The intuition (Efros / Tolstoy)

"All good matches are alike; every bad match is bad in its own way." Inliers all agree with each other and pile onto the same model; outliers disagree with everyone, so they never form a consensus. RANSAC has guarantees only when outliers are < 50%.

Pros

Simple, general, applies to many problems (line/plane/homography fitting, fundamental matrix), often works well in practice.

Cons

Parameters to tune (threshold, #iterations); can need many iterations; fails at very low inlier ratios.

Number of rounds: chosen from the expected outlier fraction and the desired success probability — more outliers or higher confidence ⇒ more iterations. The inlier threshold reflects the expected inlier noise (often modelled as Gaussian, e.g. σ ≈ 3 px). Applications named in class: image alignment / panorama stitching, 3D reconstruction, motion/translation estimation, homography estimation.

Key takeaways

◆ Remember these
  • Local features = detect → describe → match; want geometric + photometric invariance.
  • SIFT: DoG scale-space extrema → localize → orientation (rotation-invariance) → 128-D descriptor.
  • DoG ≈ LoG; extrema in \((x,y,\sigma)\) give blobs at their characteristic scale.
  • RANSAC: sample minimal set → fit → count inliers → repeat; robust if outliers < 50%.
