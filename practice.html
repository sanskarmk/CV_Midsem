PQ1 · Resolution, storage & log transform 5M

(a) A \(512\times512\) image is quantized to 6 bits per pixel. State the number of gray levels and the uncompressed storage in KB. 2M

(b) For an 8-bit image, choose \(c\) so a log transform maps the maximum input to 255, then compute the output for \(r=50\) (natural log). 2M

(c) Why does the log transform expand dark regions while compressing bright ones? 1M

Solution

(a) Gray levels
\(L = 2^{6} = \mathbf{64}\) levels.
(a) Storage
\(512\times512\times6 = 1{,}572{,}864\) bits \(\div 8 = 196{,}608\) bytes \(= \mathbf{192\ \text{KB}}\).
(b) Find c
\(c = \dfrac{255}{\ln(1+255)} = \dfrac{255}{\ln 256} = \dfrac{255}{5.545} = 45.99\).
(b) Apply at r = 50
\(s = 45.99\,\ln(1+50) = 45.99\,\ln 51 = 45.99\times 3.932 = 180.8 \approx \mathbf{181}\).
(c) Reason
The slope of \(\log(1+r)\) is large for small \(r\) and small for large \(r\). So a fixed input step near 0 produces a big output change (dark detail spread out), while the same step near 255 produces a tiny output change (bright values squeezed together).
Answers

(a) 64 levels, 192 KB.  (b) \(c\approx45.99\), \(s\approx181\).  (c) Log's decreasing slope expands low intensities and compresses high ones.

PQ3 · Sobel gradient & direction 5M

For the patch \(\begin{bmatrix}20&20&20\\20&20&20\\120&120&120\end{bmatrix}\):

(a) Compute \(g_x\) and \(g_y\) at the centre using the Sobel operators. 3M

(b) Find the gradient magnitude, gradient direction, and the edge orientation. 2M

Solution

(a) gx = (right col · [1,2,1]) − (left col · [1,2,1])
columns are identical (20,20,120), so \(g_x = (20{+}40{+}120) - (20{+}40{+}120) = \mathbf{0}\).
(a) gy = (bottom row · [1,2,1]) − (top row · [1,2,1])
\((120{+}240{+}120) - (20{+}40{+}20) = 480 - 80 = \mathbf{400}\).
(b) Magnitude
\(\|\nabla f\| = \sqrt{0^2 + 400^2} = \mathbf{400}\).
(b) Gradient direction
\(\theta = \tan^{-1}(g_y/g_x) = \tan^{-1}(400/0) = \mathbf{90^\circ}\) (points straight up/down).
(b) Edge orientation
edge ⟂ gradient \(= 90^\circ - 90^\circ = \mathbf{0^\circ}\) → a horizontal edge.
Answer

\(g_x=0,\ g_y=400\); magnitude 400; gradient at \(90^\circ\); the edge is horizontal — consistent with the dark-top / bright-bottom patch.

PQ4 · Laplacian & operators 5M

Every row of a 5×5 image is \([100,100,20,20,20]\) (a vertical edge between columns 1 and 2).

(a) Compute the Laplacian at the bright-side edge pixel (value 100, with the dark column 20 on its right) using both \(K_1=[0,1,0;1,-4,1;0,1,0]\) and \(K_2=[1,1,1;1,-8,1;1,1,1]\). 3M

(b) Compare first-derivative and second-derivative edge operators. 2M

Solution

(a) Identify neighbours
centre = 100; up = down = left = 100; right = 20.
(a) K₁ (4-neighbour)
\(-4(100) + (100{+}100{+}100{+}20) = -400 + 320 = \mathbf{-80}\).
(a) K₂ (8-neighbour)
8 neighbours = five 100's + three 20's = \(560\); \(\;-8(100) + 560 = -800 + 560 = \mathbf{-240}\).
(b) Compare
First-derivative (Roberts/Prewitt/Sobel): edge at gradient maxima, gives magnitude & direction, more noise-robust (esp. Sobel). Second-derivative (Laplacian): edge at zero-crossings, isotropic, no direction, very noise-sensitive (so usually used as LoG after Gaussian smoothing).
Answer

\(K_1=-80,\ K_2=-240\) (both negative on the bright side; they'd be \(+80,+240\) on the dark side → zero-crossing at the edge). \(K_2\) responds ~3× stronger. First-derivative ops give direction and resist noise better; second-derivative ops localize via zero-crossings but amplify noise.

PQ5 · Hough transform — both spaces 5M

(a) Using the Hough \((m,c)\) transform, determine whether \((0,1)\), \((1,3)\) and \((2,5)\) are collinear, and give the line. 3M

(b) Give the \(\rho\text{–}\theta\) representation of \((3,0)\) and \((0,3)\), and find the line passing through both. 2M

Solution

(a) Vote-lines \(c = -x m + y\)
\((0,1)\!: c = 1;\quad (1,3)\!: c = -m+3;\quad (2,5)\!: c = -2m+5\).
(a) Intersect
From the first, \(c=1\). Sub into the second: \(-m+3 = 1 \Rightarrow m = 2\), so \((m,c)=(2,1)\).
(a) Verify with the third
\(-2(2)+5 = 1\) ✓ → all three meet at \((2,1)\).
(b) Sinusoids \(\rho = x\cos\theta + y\sin\theta\)
\((3,0)\!: \rho = 3\cos\theta;\quad (0,3)\!: \rho = 3\sin\theta\).
(b) Intersect
\(3\cos\theta = 3\sin\theta \Rightarrow \tan\theta = 1 \Rightarrow \theta = 45^\circ\); then \(\rho = 3\cos45^\circ = 3/\sqrt2 \approx 2.12\).
Answers

(a) Collinear at \((m,c)=(2,1)\) → line \(\mathbf{y = 2x + 1}\).  (b) \((\rho,\theta)=(2.12,\,45^\circ)\), i.e. \(x\cos45^\circ + y\sin45^\circ = 2.12\) → line \(\mathbf{x + y = 3}\).

PQ6 · Harris, HOG & RANSAC 5M

(a) A window gives \(\sum I_x^2 = 5,\ \sum I_y^2 = 5,\ \sum I_xI_y = 2\). With \(k=0.04\), compute the Harris response \(R\) and classify the region. 2M

(b) A pixel has gradient direction \(125^\circ\) and magnitude \(60\). Distribute its vote across the 9 HOG bins. 2M

(c) For RANSAC line fitting (\(s=2\)) with 30% outliers, how many iterations give 99% success? 1M

Solution

(a) det & trace
\(M=\begin{bmatrix}5&2\\2&5\end{bmatrix}\): \(\det = 25 - 4 = 21\), \(\text{trace} = 10\).
(a) Response
\(R = 21 - 0.04(10)^2 = 21 - 4 = \mathbf{17} > 0\) → corner (eigenvalues \(7\) and \(3\), both large).
(b) Locate 125°
between bin centres 120° and 140°.
(b) Weights & distribute
to 120°: \((140-125)/20 = 0.75 \Rightarrow 0.75\times60 = 45\); to 140°: \((125-120)/20 = 0.25 \Rightarrow 0.25\times60 = 15\).
(c) RANSAC iterations
\((1-0.3)^2 = 0.49\); \(N = \dfrac{\log(0.01)}{\log(1-0.49)} = \dfrac{-2}{\log 0.51} = \dfrac{-2}{-0.292} \approx 6.8\).
Answers

(a) \(R=17>0\) → corner.  (b) Bin 120° = 45, Bin 140° = 15, rest 0.  (c) \(N\approx \mathbf{7}\) iterations.

Rapid-fire theory (predicted)

Short, high-probability theory prompts. Try to answer in 1–2 lines before revealing.

T1 Define sampling and quantization. 2M
Answer

Sampling = digitizing spatial coordinates \((x,y)\) (the pixel grid / spatial resolution). Quantization = digitizing the intensity into \(L=2^b\) gray levels (intensity resolution).

T2 How do convolution and correlation differ? 2M
Answer

Convolution flips the kernel \(180^\circ\) before the multiply-and-add; correlation does not. They coincide for symmetric kernels. Convolution is commutative and associative, enabling pre-combination of filters (e.g. derivative-of-Gaussian).

T3 List the four steps of the Canny edge detector. 2M
Answer

① Gaussian smoothing; ② gradient magnitude & angle (Sobel); ③ non-maximum suppression (thin to 1 px along the gradient); ④ double-threshold + hysteresis linking (\(T_H:T_L\) ≈ 2:1–3:1).

T4 Why two thresholds in Canny? 1M
Answer

A high threshold confidently starts edges (rejects noise); a low threshold continues them where connected to a strong edge — bridging gaps without admitting noise. One threshold can't do both.

T5 Why is the polar \(\rho\text{–}\theta\) Hough form preferred over \(m\text{–}c\)? 1M
Answer

Vertical lines have infinite slope, which \(m\text{–}c\) can't store in a finite accumulator. In \(\rho\text{–}\theta\) both parameters are bounded (\(\theta\in[0,180^\circ)\), \(\rho\in[-D,D]\)), so every line fits.

T6 Differentiate an edge from a corner (structure-tensor view). 2M
Answer

Edge: strong gradient in one direction → one large, one small eigenvalue of \(M\) → \(R<0\); poorly localized along the edge (aperture problem). Corner: gradients in two directions → both eigenvalues large → \(R>0\); uniquely localizable and great for matching.

T7 What is the Difference of Gaussians and why does SIFT use it? 2M
Answer

DoG \(= G(k\sigma){*}I - G(\sigma){*}I\) approximates the scale-normalised Laplacian-of-Gaussian (a blob detector) but is nearly free to compute from the Gaussian pyramid. Local extrema in \((x,y,\sigma)\) give keypoints at their characteristic scale → scale invariance.

T8 State the RGB↔CMYK relationship and why each is used. 2M
Answer

\(C=1-R,\ M=1-G,\ Y=1-B\); \(K=\min(C,M,Y)\). RGB is additive (screens emit light, all-on=white); CMYK is subtractive (inks absorb light on white paper, all-on=black). The physical process dictates the model.
