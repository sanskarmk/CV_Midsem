Harris Corner Detector & HOG

Corners are the most distinctive local features — stable under rotation, scale and lighting. Harris finds them from the structure tensor's eigenvalues. Then HOG turns local gradient orientations into a descriptor robust enough to detect pedestrians.

Why corners?

A corner is a point where intensity changes significantly in both directions — typically where two edges meet. Corners matter because they are highly distinctive and invariant to rotation, scale and illumination changes, which makes them excellent, repeatable landmarks for image matching, panorama stitching, tracking, camera calibration, 3D reconstruction, SLAM and AR.

The key idea: flat vs edge vs corner

Slide a small window over the image and ask: how much does the content change if I shift the window a little in any direction?

RegionShift the window…Intensity change
Flatin any directionNo change in any direction
Edgealong the edge / across itNo change along the edge; big change across it
Cornerin any directionBig change in every direction
◆ One line to remember

"We should easily recognise a corner by looking through a small window, because shifting the window in any direction gives a large change in intensity." That uniqueness is what makes corners matchable.

Harris mathematics

Quantify "change when shifting by \((u,v)\)" as a weighted sum of squared differences:

\[ E(u,v) = \sum_{x,y} w(x,y)\,\big[\,I(x+u,\,y+v) - I(x,y)\,\big]^2 \]

For small shifts, a first-order Taylor expansion gives the quadratic form

\[ E(u,v) \approx \begin{bmatrix} u & v \end{bmatrix}\, M\, \begin{bmatrix} u \\ v \end{bmatrix}, \qquad M = \sum_{x,y} w(x,y)\begin{bmatrix} I_x^2 & I_x I_y \\ I_x I_y & I_y^2 \end{bmatrix} \]

\(M\) is the structure tensor (second-moment matrix). It summarises the distribution of gradients inside the window. Its two eigenvalues \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2\) measure how much the intensity varies along the two principal directions:

EigenvaluesGradient scatterRegion
Both smallclustered at originFlat
One large, one smallspread along a lineEdge
Both largespread in 2-DCorner

The corner response R

Computing eigenvalues at every pixel is expensive, so Harris uses a cheap response that depends only on \(\det\) and \(\text{trace}\):

\[ R = \det(M) - k\,[\,\text{trace}(M)\,]^2 = \lambda_1\lambda_2 - k(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)^2, \qquad k \in [0.04, 0.06] \]
ResponseMeans
\(R > 0\) (large)Corner — both eigenvalues large
\(R < 0\) (large magnitude)Edge — one eigenvalue dominates
\(|R|\) smallFlat — both eigenvalues small

The algorithm: ① compute \(R\) everywhere; ② keep points with \(R > \text{threshold}\); ③ take only the local maxima of \(R\) (non-max suppression) so each corner is reported once.

Harris numerical

Once the gradient sums in a window are known, \(R\) is a quick plug-in. Compare a corner-like window against an edge-like one (\(k=0.04\)):

Corner window

\(\sum I_x^2 = 2,\ \sum I_y^2 = 2,\ \sum I_xI_y = 0\)

\(M=\begin{bmatrix}2&0\\0&2\end{bmatrix}\), \(\det=4\), \(\text{trace}=4\)

\(R = 4 - 0.04(4)^2 = 4 - 0.64 = \mathbf{3.36} > 0\)

\(\lambda_1=\lambda_2=2\) (both large) → corner.

Edge window

\(\sum I_x^2 = 2,\ \sum I_y^2 = 0,\ \sum I_xI_y = 0\)

\(M=\begin{bmatrix}2&0\\0&0\end{bmatrix}\), \(\det=0\), \(\text{trace}=2\)

\(R = 0 - 0.04(2)^2 = \mathbf{-0.16} < 0\)

\(\lambda_1=2,\lambda_2=0\) (one large) → edge.

✦ Difference between an edge and a corner (Q5b)

An edge has a strong gradient in one direction only — the structure tensor has one large and one small eigenvalue, so \(R < 0\). A corner has strong gradients in two directions — both eigenvalues are large, so \(R > 0\). That's why corners are localizable (well-defined in 2D) while edges suffer the "aperture problem" (you can't tell where you are along the edge).

Histogram of Oriented Gradients (HOG)

HOG is a feature descriptor for object detection (famously pedestrians). The idea: local object shape is well captured by the distribution of gradient orientations. Steps:

Gradients
Compute \(g_x, g_y\) (filter masks \([-1,0,1]\) and its transpose), then magnitude \(\sqrt{g_x^2+g_y^2}\) and orientation \(\tan^{-1}(g_y/g_x)\) at each pixel.
Cells
Over a detection patch of 64×128, divide into 8×8-pixel cells. For each cell, build a histogram of gradient orientations into 9 bins (0–180°), each pixel voting by its magnitude.
Blocks & normalization
Group cells into 2×2 blocks that slide with overlap. Normalise each block's concatenated histogram (e.g. L2-norm) to cancel lighting/contrast changes.
Concatenate
Stack all normalised block histograms into one feature vector. For 64×128: 7×15 blocks × 4 cells × 9 bins = 3780 dimensions. Feed to a classifier (e.g. SVM).

Key takeaways

◆ Remember these
  • Structure tensor \(M=\sum w\begin{bmatrix}I_x^2&I_xI_y\\I_xI_y&I_y^2\end{bmatrix}\); eigenvalues classify flat/edge/corner.
  • Corner response \(R=\det M - k(\text{trace }M)^2\), \(k\approx0.04\!-\!0.06\): \(R{>}0\) corner, \(R{<}0\) edge, \(|R|\) small flat.
  • HOG: 8×8 cells → 9 orientation bins → 2×2 blocks (overlapping) → normalise → concatenate → 3780-D for 64×128.
  • Edge = 1 dominant gradient direction; corner = 2 → corners are uniquely localizable.
